Deep learning has become an increasingly visible tool in mathematical finance,
including derivative pricing, risk management, and hedging. Among these applications,
hedging is particularly important because it sits at the interface between theoretical
finance and practical trading: a hedging rule must perform under uncertainty, discrete
trading, model error, and---in realistic settings---transaction costs and constraints.

Classical derivative theory provides a natural benchmark. In complete and frictionless
models such as Black--Scholes \citep{BlackScholes1973, Merton1973}, a contingent claim
can be replicated exactly by continuous-time dynamic trading, yielding a closed-form
pricing and hedging framework. However, the assumptions required for perfect
replication are restrictive. In practice, hedging is discrete, transaction costs
matter, volatility is not constant, and the true data-generating mechanism is unknown.
These features motivate the study of imperfect hedging and, more recently,
data-driven or learning-based hedge construction.

This review surveys the literature on deep learning for derivative hedging, with
particular emphasis on the transition from neural networks used for \emph{pricing} to
neural networks used for \emph{direct hedge generation}. Rather than presenting papers
in chronological order, we organise the literature into thematic groups: foundational
approaches to imperfect hedging; early neural-network methods for pricing and
sensitivity estimation; modern deep hedging frameworks that learn trading policies
directly; and recent extensions involving reinforcement learning, rough volatility,
market frictions, and data-driven scenario generation.

The aim is not merely to list contributions, but to evaluate what these approaches
actually achieve, under which assumptions they appear strongest, and where their
weaknesses remain. In particular, we ask whether neural methods deliver genuine
economic improvements over classical benchmarks, or whether apparent gains are
sometimes driven by narrow simulation choices, favourable objectives, or unrealistic
information structures.
